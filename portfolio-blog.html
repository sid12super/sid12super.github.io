A retrospective on two years in the MS Applied Data Science program at Syracuse University's iSchool, linking coursework, research, and projects to the program's six learning outcomes.

01

Program Learning Outcomes

01
Collect, Store, and Access Data
Identify and leverage applicable technologies to collect, store, and retrieve data at scale. This means understanding databases, cloud storage, APIs, and data pipelines, and knowing when to use which tool for a given data problem.
Genomic Analysis (S3) GenNext (APIs) Soccer DBMS
02
Create Actionable Insight
Use data and the full data science life cycle to generate insights that drive real decisions across societal, business, and political contexts. Not just analysis for its own sake, but insight that someone can act on.
Hallucination Study Diabetes Risk Energy Analysis
03
Visualization and Predictive Models
Apply visualization techniques and predictive modeling to surface patterns and generate forward-looking insight. This spans everything from EDA with matplotlib to building neural networks that predict real outcomes.
Diabetes Risk (DNN) Betting Edge (DQN) Energy Analysis
04
Programming Languages (R and Python)
Use R and Python to support the full pipeline from data ingestion to model deployment. Both languages appeared throughout the program: Python as the primary tool for ML and NLP work, R for statistical analysis and visualization.
All Projects (Python) Energy Analysis (R)
05
Communicate Insights
Present findings clearly to both technical and non-technical audiences, including sponsors and team leads. This includes dashboards, presentations, written reports, and the ability to translate model outputs into business language.
Betting Edge (Streamlit) Hallucination Study Soccer DBMS (Power BI)
06
Ethics in Data Science
Apply fairness, bias mitigation, transparency, and privacy considerations in the development and evaluation of data systems and predictive models. Not an afterthought, but a design constraint.
Betting Edge (Ethical Nets) Hallucination Study

Track Selection

Artificial Intelligence

Covering deep learning, reinforcement learning, and multi-agent architectures. Projects like Betting Edge (DQN) and Genomic Analysis (CNN on AWS) directly apply AI techniques to real-world problems at scale.

Natural Language Processing

From BERT-based sentiment analysis at NEXIS Lab to the Financial LLM Hallucination Study, NLP has been central to my work. This track gave me the foundation for RAG systems, prompt engineering, and LLM evaluation.

Data Pipelines & ELT

Building reliable data infrastructure: OAuth2 API integrations at GenNext.Mortgage, Dask-based preprocessing on AWS S3, FAISS vector stores, and MCP server integration for automated research workflows.

04

Reflections

What I Expected

When I applied to the MS in Applied Data Science at Syracuse, I was looking for a research-oriented program that would give me both theoretical depth and practical exposure. Coming from an Electronics and Telecommunications background at the University of Mumbai, I knew I wanted to transition fully into data science and AI, and I needed a program that would take that seriously.

The program delivered on that promise. Syracuse's iSchool didn't just teach me algorithms in isolation; it placed me in research labs, connected me with faculty who were building real systems, and gave me project exposure across almost every domain I was curious about. That breadth turned out to be one of the most valuable things about the degree.

What I Actually Learned

PLO 1, Collect, Store, and Access Data: My first real encounter with data infrastructure at scale came during the Genomic Analysis project, where I had to process 100K+ FASTA files on AWS S3 using Dask for parallel preprocessing. At GenNext.Mortgage, I built OAuth2 API integrations that automated mortgage data extraction for 200+ daily loan records. The Soccer DBMS project grounded me in relational database design with SQL and Azure. Each project taught a different layer of the data stack.

PLO 2, Actionable Insight: The Hallucination Study was where this clicked. Benchmarking six LLMs on financial QA tasks wasn't just an academic exercise; the finding that Claude achieved 0% hallucination rate while GPT-5 hit 40% is the kind of insight a financial services firm could act on immediately. The Diabetes Risk Assessment similarly produced actionable results: identifying that socio-economic variables alone can predict diabetes risk with an F1-score of 0.57 has direct implications for public health screening.

PLO 3, Visualization and Predictive Models: I built predictive models across nearly every project: Dense Neural Networks for diabetes risk, CNNs for genomic classification, DQN agents for sports analytics, and BERT transformers for sentiment analysis. Visualization ranged from Streamlit dashboards (Hallucination Study, Betting Edge) to Power BI (Soccer DBMS) to matplotlib/seaborn EDA across all analytical projects. The key lesson was that a model is only as useful as its presentation.

PLO 4, Programming Languages: Python was the backbone of everything: TensorFlow, LangChain, Scikit-learn, pandas, NLTK, Streamlit. R appeared primarily in the Energy Analysis project (IST 687) for statistical analysis and visualization. SQL was essential for the Soccer DBMS and database management coursework. JavaScript and Google Apps Script came into play during the GenNext internship.

PLO 5, Communicate Insights: Betting Edge had a full Streamlit interface designed for non-technical users to understand risk profiles. The Hallucination Study included comparison dashboards that made complex LLM evaluation metrics accessible. The Soccer DBMS featured a Power BI dashboard and an MS Power App for interactive team performance exploration. Throughout the program, I learned that the ability to explain model outputs to stakeholders is as important as building the model itself.

PLO 6, Ethics: This was most deeply explored in Betting Edge, where I implemented Ethical Nets and fairness constraints within the agent logic using a fine-tuned LLM. The system was designed to mitigate algorithmic bias and incorporate responsible gambling guardrails, not as a feature bolted on at the end, but as a core architectural constraint. The Hallucination Study also touched on ethics: evaluating whether LLMs can be trusted for financial advice is fundamentally a question about responsible AI deployment.

Favorite Class

IST 688, Human-Centered AI Applications, taught by Prof. Jeffrey Saltz, the Program Director. This was the most anticipated class on my list, and it delivered exactly how the learning outcomes were structured. The progression from basic LLM applications to building multi-agent systems under his mentorship, with weekly app-building assignments, gave me a structured path to go from "I can use an API" to "I can architect an agentic system." Betting Edge was the capstone of that journey.

The Internship

My internship at GenNext.Mortgage gave me my first real look at what the corporate world expects. Working at a startup, the intensity hit immediately after my first task submission and review under the CEO. The constant cycle of building, failing, getting feedback, and improving taught me more about professional resilience than any class could. The technical outcome, an automated pricing pipeline using OAuth2 and Google Apps Script, ended up helping the company reach more customers. But the real takeaway was learning how to operate under pressure and deliver solutions that actually get used.

What Surprised Me

Three things I didn't expect. First, how much I learned through collaboration. Working with teammates like Aryan, Ameya, and Gautam on Betting Edge, and with Shruti and Saketh on the Hallucination Study, taught me that the best technical decisions come from arguing through tradeoffs with people who see the problem differently.

Second, the facilities and resources on campus. Coming from Mumbai, the scale of what Syracuse offers, from research labs to computing infrastructure, was a significant step up.

Third, the multilingual and multicultural exposure. Working with classmates from diverse backgrounds didn't just broaden my worldview; it made me a better collaborator and communicator.

Advice for Incoming Students

Lock in from Day 1. The program moves fast, and the students who get the most out of it are the ones who start building immediately. Don't wait for permission to start a project. Experience new things. Get the right exposure. Meet new people. Collaborate. Set both research-oriented and corporate goals, because the program gives you a runway for both. Good luck.

Synthesis

Looking back across two years, what this program taught me wasn't just a set of skills; it was how to integrate them. Any single project touches multiple PLOs: Betting Edge alone covers all six. But the real growth was learning to see a problem (like LLM unreliability in finance, or diabetes prediction from non-clinical data) and knowing which combination of data engineering, modeling, visualization, and communication to apply. The program gave me the building blocks. The projects taught me how to put them together. And the people I worked with taught me that the best work happens in collaboration.
